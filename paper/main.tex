\begin{abstract}
Most LLM routing systems are \emph{supervised}: routers are trained on human preferences, win/loss labels, or reward-model scores.
We ask whether \emph{unsupervised signals}---computed from the query and from model responses \emph{without} routing supervision at extraction time---can guide \textbf{binary LLM routing}: selecting the appropriate model from a fixed capability-differentiated pool.
We define oracle routing need $r(q)$, three signal layers by \textbf{information availability}, analyze which predict $r(q)$ on calib, freeze $x(q)$, and route via \textbf{binary model-selection} policy $\pi(q)$ from $s(q)=\lambda^\top x(q)$ (Section~\ref{sec:routing-policy}) without observing $r(q)$ online.
Our pool contains two models ($\Mlo$, $\Mhi$); routing reduces to a binary decision.
We compare hand-designed and learned weights on calib (H4) and report accuracy--cost Pareto curves on test.
