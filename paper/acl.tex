\begin{abstract}
Multi-LLM systems must trade off response quality against inference cost, yet most routing methods rely on supervised routers, learned pre-hoc estimators, or information available only after full answer generation.
We study \textbf{routing based on unsupervised pre-inference signals}: inexpensive model-independent features and model-dependent probe statistics extracted before complete generation.
Our primary contribution is \textbf{signal extraction and characterization}---how much routing-relevant information these signals carry (Studies~I--II), whether the families complement each other (Study~III), and whether a simple calibrated policy can exploit whatever information exists (Study~IV).
Signal computation is unsupervised; oracle labels from offline full generation are used only to evaluate informativeness and to calibrate the demonstration policy on a validation split---never to extract signals.
On ARC-Challenge ($n{=}299$ calibration, $n{=}1{,}172$ test), we report per-family informativeness, $\Delta$AUROC across families, and cost--quality utility against static baselines.
We do not present a deployable routing product; negative or uniformly null signal findings are reported as empirical limits under our protocol.
